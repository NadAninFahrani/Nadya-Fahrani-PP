Abraham Maslow

Hierchy of Needs

A psychological theory known as Abraham Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs proposes a five-tier model of human needs that can be viewed as a pyramid.

According to Maslow's theory, people are driven to meet their needs in a hierarchical order, with lower-level requirements needing to be met before moving on to higher-level demands.

In this hierarchy, most humans have already gotten the 1st and 2nd layer, but most lack love and self-esteem due to their surroundings and overall view of life.

Where it gets dangerous is when we turn to only social media for love and praise because we want to feel like we belong and to feel appreciated. Even though the comments (praise) arent face-to-face, it still means the same amount as if it was.

Looking for constant praise and stimulus will make you comfortable with this feeling, thus making it a habit. When one makes an action a habit because of an emotional stimuli, they'll feel uneasy when it's out of their daily practice.

The feeling of giving into those urges will make you feel a sort of satisfaction known as the dopamine peak.

The early stages of giving into this urge will make those peaks very high but as you progress you'll start to feel less of the highs

Because we always go to social media for the highs, our dopamine levels won't have enough time to refill

What we need to take note of is that teenagers' prefrontal cortex, the part that makes all the decision making, isn't fully developed until they're 25.

Prefrontal Cortex

The prefrontal cortex is the front part of the brain, behind the forehead, known for executive functions like decision-making, personality expression, and social behavior. It regulates attention, working memory, and emotional responses, playing a vital role in complex cognitive processes.

Amygdala

A teenager's amygdala, which is their emotional controls, are already fully developed when they're adolescents.

The brain's temporal lobes contain two almond-shaped structures, one on each side, that are known as the amygdala. It is an essential part of the limbic system, a collection of neural structures related to behavior, emotions, and memory. In particular, the processing of emotions pertaining to fear and pleasure is greatly aided by the amygdala.

This means that teenagers think with emotions rather than logic because they're quite literally wired to do that. Once a teenager finds something stimulating, they are most likely going to be using it to make sure they feel “happy” constantly.
